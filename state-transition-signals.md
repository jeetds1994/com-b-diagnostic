# State Transition Signals

## What this file is

Observable, falsifiable indicators for each transition in the 7-state behavior diagnostic cycle. These signals tell you whether an intervention is working (behavior moving forward), stalling, or regressing.

Use this file after selecting interventions (analysis template Step 7) to define success criteria and track progress. Each transition includes forward signals (behavior improving), regression signals (behavior sliding back), and measurement suggestions.

---

## How to read the transitions

The 7-state cycle flows:

```
Contested/Undefined -> Aspirational Only -> Weakly Realized -> Partially Realized
-> Realized but Friction-Filled -> Fully Realized & Stable -> Actively Suppressed
-> (back to Contested/Undefined)
```

Each section below describes the transition FROM one state TO the next forward state.

---

## Transition 1: Contested/Undefined -> Aspirational Only

**What is happening:** The behavior moves from "we can't even agree on what this is" to "we agree this matters, even if we don't do it yet."

### Forward Signals
- Actors use consistent terminology when describing the behavior
- A shared definition exists (even if informal) that multiple actors reference
- Leadership or influential actors publicly endorse the behavior
- Debates shift from "what is this?" to "how should we do this?"

### Regression Signals
- New actors enter the system and reintroduce competing definitions
- A failed attempt to standardize triggers backlash and fragmentation
- Vocabulary diverges again under pressure

### Measurement Suggestions
- **Qualitative:** Interview actors — can they describe the behavior in compatible terms?
- **Quantitative:** If the tool has a glossary or shared definitions, track adoption (views, references)

### Avalanche Observation Example
- **Forward:** Field observers, patrol, and forecasters agree on what an "observation" is and what fields matter. Conversations shift from "should we standardize reporting?" to "how should the form work?"
- **Regression:** A new observer cohort joins with different training backgrounds and uses terms differently; "observation" means different things to different groups again.

## Transition 2: Aspirational Only -> Weakly Realized

**What is happening:** The behavior moves from "we want to do this" to "we actually do it sometimes, but it keeps getting displaced."

### Forward Signals
- First real instances of the behavior appear (not just plans or pilots)
- Some actors perform the behavior without being asked
- Infrastructure exists (even if minimal) — forms, tools, channels
- The behavior shows up in workflows, not just in strategy documents

### Regression Signals
- Early adopters burn out or lose interest without reinforcement
- Symbolic endorsement continues but no infrastructure is built
- The behavior remains in plans and presentations but not in practice

### Measurement Suggestions
- **Quantitative:** Count instances of the behavior per week/month. Any non-zero number is progress.
- **Qualitative:** Are actors who perform the behavior doing it voluntarily or only when prompted?

### Avalanche Observation Example
- **Forward:** A handful of observers start using the reporting app. Submissions trickle in. The forecaster checks the app occasionally. The behavior is real but sporadic.
- **Regression:** The app is launched but after initial excitement, usage drops to near-zero. It remains "the thing we should be using" without becoming part of anyone's routine.

---

## Transition 3: Weakly Realized -> Partially Realized / Inconsistent

**What is happening:** The behavior moves from "it happens sometimes" to "it happens regularly but differently across actors/teams."

### Forward Signals
- Multiple actors perform the behavior regularly (not just early adopters)
- The behavior is part of some teams' routines but not others
- Local best practices emerge (even if they conflict across groups)
- Actors expect the behavior to happen (it's no longer surprising)

### Regression Signals
- Key actors stop performing the behavior when workload increases
- The behavior stays confined to early adopters and doesn't spread
- Competing priorities consistently win ("we'll get to it when things calm down")

### Measurement Suggestions
- **Quantitative:** Track behavior frequency across actors/teams. Look for variance — high variance confirms "partially realized."
- **Quantitative:** Measure the gap between highest-performing and lowest-performing teams.
- **Qualitative:** Do actors describe the behavior as "what we do" or "what we should do"?

### Avalanche Observation Example
- **Forward:** Professional observers submit daily. Ski patrol submits when they remember. Guides submit occasionally after notable days. Each group has its own habits. The forecaster checks submissions every morning but coverage is uneven.
- **Regression:** Submission frequency drops during high-workload periods (storm cycles, holidays). The behavior is routine only for the most committed observers.

---

## Transition 4: Partially Realized -> Realized but Friction-Filled

**What is happening:** The behavior moves from "done differently by different people" to "done consistently, but painfully."

### Forward Signals
- A canonical method or standard emerges (actors converge on one way)
- The behavior is expected of everyone, not just enthusiasts
- Compliance is high but complaints about friction are also high
- Workarounds exist — actors do the behavior but route around pain points
- Training or onboarding includes the behavior as standard practice

### Regression Signals
- Standardization attempt fails and teams revert to local methods
- A new tool or process is introduced that fragments the standard
- Experienced actors opt out of the standard ("I'll do it my way")

### Measurement Suggestions
- **Quantitative:** Variance across teams decreases. Completion rate increases. Time-to-complete may increase (friction).
- **Quantitative:** Track workaround frequency — are actors using the system as designed, or routing around it?
- **Qualitative:** Do actors describe the behavior as "painful but necessary" vs. "one of several options"?

### Avalanche Observation Example
- **Forward:** All observers use the same form. Submissions happen reliably. But observers complain about form length, connectivity issues, and the time it takes. Forecasters get consistent data but the triage process is manual and slow. The system works — it just hurts.
- **Regression:** A new reporting format is introduced alongside the existing one. Some observers switch, some don't. The brief period of consistency fragments.

---

## Transition 5: Realized but Friction-Filled -> Fully Realized & Stable

**What is happening:** The behavior moves from "works but hurts" to "works smoothly and is trusted."

### Forward Signals
- Friction complaints decrease or disappear
- The behavior becomes habitual — actors do it without deliberate effort
- Outputs are trusted by downstream consumers without manual verification
- The behavior has clear ownership and is maintained as infrastructure
- New actors adopt the behavior quickly (low onboarding friction)
- The behavior is resilient to disruption (continues during high-pressure periods)

### Regression Signals
- A system change (tool migration, reorg, personnel turnover) disrupts the routine
- Friction is reduced but at the cost of quality (shortcuts become defaults)
- Ownership becomes unclear as the behavior is "everyone's job" (and therefore no one's)

### Measurement Suggestions
- **Quantitative:** Time-to-complete decreases. Completion rate is high and stable. Error/rejection rates are low.
- **Quantitative:** Measure resilience — does behavior frequency hold during peak periods (storm cycles)?
- **Qualitative:** Do actors describe the behavior as "just what we do" vs. "something we have to do"?

### Avalanche Observation Example
- **Forward:** Observers submit quickly via mobile. GPS and timestamps are automatic. Forms are fast. Forecasters receive structured, queryable data with spatial metadata. Observations flow into the forecast cycle reliably. Coverage gaps are visible and addressed. The system is trusted.
- **Regression:** A tool migration disrupts the routine. Or: the lead forecaster who championed the system leaves, and the new one doesn't maintain the same triage rigor. The behavior continues but quality drifts.

## Transition 6: Fully Realized & Stable -> Actively Suppressed

**What is happening:** External forces begin to actively prevent a behavior that was previously stable. This is not natural drift — it is suppression.

### Forward Signals (suppression occurring)
- Policy or organizational changes explicitly discourage the behavior
- Incentive structures shift to penalize or ignore the behavior
- Key enablers (tools, budget, time allocation) are removed
- Actors who perform the behavior face social or professional consequences
- The behavior goes underground (performed secretly or informally)

### Resistance Signals (suppression being resisted)
- Actors continue the behavior despite pressure to stop
- Informal workarounds preserve the behavior outside official channels
- Vocal advocates push back against the suppressive forces

### Measurement Suggestions
- **Quantitative:** Sudden drop in behavior frequency that correlates with a policy/org change (not a gradual decline)
- **Qualitative:** Do actors describe external reasons for stopping? ("We were told to..." vs. "It just faded")

### Avalanche Observation Example
- **Forward (suppression):** Budget cuts eliminate observer positions. A new management directive prioritizes speed over data quality, discouraging thorough observations. Liability concerns make organizations reluctant to share observations across boundaries.
- **Resistance:** Individual observers continue submitting because they believe it matters, even without institutional support. Data sharing moves to informal channels (text messages, WhatsApp groups).

---

## Transition 7: Actively Suppressed -> Contested/Undefined

**What is happening:** Prolonged suppression erodes shared understanding. Actors no longer agree on what the behavior is or whether it matters.

### Forward Signals (dissolution occurring)
- New actors enter who have no memory of the behavior being practiced
- Competing definitions emerge ("what we used to do" vs. "what we should do now")
- The behavior's purpose is questioned or reframed
- Institutional memory of how the behavior worked is lost

### Recovery Signals (potential re-emergence)
- A crisis or failure exposes the gap left by the suppressed behavior
- New leadership or external pressure creates space for the behavior to return
- Actors begin debating the behavior's definition again — a precursor to re-establishing it

### Measurement Suggestions
- **Qualitative:** Can actors describe the behavior consistently? If not, you're in Contested/Undefined territory.
- **Quantitative:** If a tool existed for the behavior, are there any active users?

### Avalanche Observation Example
- **Forward (dissolution):** After years of underfunding, new observers have never used a structured reporting system. "Observation reporting" means different things — some think it's a formal snowpack profile, others think it's a trip report on social media, others think it's a verbal radio call. The original shared standard has been lost.
- **Recovery:** A major avalanche incident reveals that critical observations existed but were never aggregated. The post-incident review recommends re-establishing a shared reporting system. The cycle begins again from "what do we even mean by observation reporting?"

## Using These Signals in Practice

### Setting success criteria
Before implementing an intervention, define which forward signals you expect to see and by when. Be specific:
- "Within 3 months, we expect X observers submitting Y observations per week" (Transition 3)
- "Within 6 months, variance in submission completeness across teams should decrease by Z%" (Transition 4)

### Detecting regression early
Monitor regression signals alongside forward signals. Regression is often invisible until it's advanced — actors rarely announce that they've stopped doing something.

### Adjusting interventions
If forward signals stall for more than one review cycle, re-diagnose:
1. Has the blocker profile changed? (Use the diagnostic cycle to re-classify the behavior state)
2. Is the intervention targeting the right COM-B code? (Check blocker concentration)
3. Is there a new interface failure that the intervention doesn't address?
